About

It was on a grey day such as this that I cast eyes on my adversary. The devil does not come in cloven hoof, horns and a tail. But in a familiar form. He stands and stares, unflinching, with all the time he pleases. And again, it matters not what form he takes. Man or woman? pay it no mind, because as the sun rises in the east and sets in the west our enemy can change his countenance to that which he pleases. But be sure of one thing, he aims to destroy you. - Father Malachi.Ret

The attachment is a spooky tea-time drama entangling the lives of five people accross three decades. Each fights their own demons, fnally resulting in a showdown with a paranormal entity that is intent on destroying their lives.

Set initially amongst the leafy suburbs of 1990s England, we follow our protagonists through various east London locations as the decades pass. The Attachment is a drama that slowly pulls you in to the lives of each character, and ultimately how they unite to confront their enemy.

An Interview with a "writer/director"

As a small independent film company our latest endeavour “The Attachment” was by far our most challenging production to date. Set across different time periods, our story begins in the leafy suburbs of London, United Kingdom during the early 1980s with our protagonist at a tender age of 6 years old. Shooting a biopic of this calibre took a lot of energy planning and good fortune. Shot entirely on location around London area, our small production team were certainly put to task in controlling every aspect of filmmaking process in order to deliver this satisfying supernatural drama. Made on a very small budget, the film has impressed many and has even one festival recognition.It’s not a film for everyone, but that people who like their horror films interesting and gentle without the traditional blood and gore.It was a passion project for first time director Clement Ofoedu, who we caught up with and spared us a few minutes for an interview.

Q: So how did you get involved in this film?

CO: The attachment was a script I wrote after making a couple of small short films. I wanted to progress onto writing a feature. It’s not a film I expect to win loads of accolades. But it was more of a passion project for me to push myself into doing something different after a battle with cancer.

Q: Oh, so you wrote it and I noticed you directed it. Was that a wise choice?

CO: Well, it served a purpose for me. Firstly, I wanted to learn the process of filmmaking especially feature, and it was highly unlikely that a first-time filmmaker not raised in the industry would be offered the Helms of a feature film. Therefore, I decided if I was going to make a feature film, I’d better write it and direct it. Directing is a lot harder than it looks.To be honest if I do say around a few more minutes and had a deeper think I may have passed on it. I just did not realise how much effort it would take. There is a reason the many films do not get finished. Money is one element that come sink your project fast, and the other is effort and harnessing the necessary skills to be able to pull it off.

Q: You mention money. In fact, there is something I wanted to ask you how much this cost?

O: Oh, the film was made on personal funds. I decided to opt for this approach rather than crowdsourcing. My reasoning was that I had to try to learn filmmaking, direct and produce at the same time was a big enough obstacle, without needing to have fun and games with a finance department when you be stressed out, trying to keep things running. Making a film is a bit like lighting a fire and letting it burn. It can be done we have to keep an eye on it and tend to rip constantly to make sure it doesn’t burn our control and cause serious damage.

Q: Really?

CO: Absolutely. When you think about it making a short film is a completely different experience. You have a few people gather together to make a film of short duration perhaps 5 to 10 minutes. Apart from preproduction which could take a few months depending on how intricate the film is, during production and principal photography, the team will only spend at Max a few days to a week together.And of course, at the other end there is postproduction, which is the different experience having its own unique challenges.Part of that is because momentum has died down. However, in the feature film you have people together in proximity for weeks or months at an end.This raises another problem and that is friction between people that normally wouldn’t be near one another. They may be good at their job, but that doesn’t always translate as being the most sociable person. Therefore, as an independent filmmaker you’re always firefighting, and desperately doing everything to dispel that friction and prevent the film set from going nuclear.One of the worst things that can happen apart from things a full firmly within health and safety, is that an actor decides to quit the film.This generally doesn’t happen but if the perfect storm is created where there are crew or other actors who constantly come into conflict, then there is a serious chance of such a problem developing.These are the subtle things you really told about in film school. As an independent filmmaker frequently you’re also the director who is tasked with not only directing the film but playing diplomat to ensure the film is completed at least as far as principal photography goes.
